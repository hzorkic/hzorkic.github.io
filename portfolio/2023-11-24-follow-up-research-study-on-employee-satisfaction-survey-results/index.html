üß† Background

Employee engagement and satisfaction is a critical factor influencing productivity and organizational success. It is not without suprise that many companies conduct annual employee engagement surverys to gather longitudinal data about the workplace they are cultivating. Company A Recognizing the significance of maintaining a happy and engaged workforce, our organization conducted an initial comprehensive employee happiness survey. The survey yielded quantitative data that provided a snapshot of the employees’ sentiments at a specific point in time.

When a senior leader on my team went over our team results in an all-hands meeting, we quickly glanced over the results and continued about normal procedures, however, I asked a few follow-up questions, opening the door for a larger team conversation. Turns out, there were no formal follow-up procedures in place for the quantitative survey to identify solutions to low-satisfaction areas.

After pitching the idea to the senior leader, I was approved to allocate a fraction of my hours every week towards conducting a follow-up study. By tracing the changes and trends observed in the survey responses, we aim to gain a nuanced understanding of the factors influencing happiness within our workforce and come up with actionable solutions to make it better.

This study goes beyond mere statistical analysis; it is a concerted effort to dive into the dynamics that shape employee contentment. The objective was not only to identify areas of improvement, but to formulate actionable strategies that enhance overall job satisfaction. Through this longitudinal approach, I aspired to implement targeted interventions that promote a positive and fulfilling workplace culture, fostering the well-being of our valued employees for years to come.

üöß Major Challenges

In our pursuit of understanding and improving employee happiness within our organization, we encountered several significant challenges during the initial phases of our research.

  1. Time constraints faced by our interviewers. Recognizing the importance of minimizing disruptions to daily work, we sought efficient strategies to collect valuable insights without unduly burdening our team.
  2. Creating many avenues for employees to provide their opinions. Some members of the team were uncomfortable speaking on certain topics in front of their co-workers, while some prefered to bounce ideas around with others. We needed to ensure inclusivity by providing multiple avenues for sharing their sentiments. This approach ensures that the survey captures a comprehensive range of perspectives, contributing to a more holistic understanding of employee happiness.
  3. Establishing trust with participants by maintaining the confidentiality and anonymity of respondents. This deliberate choice aimed to eliminate potential biases that could arise from higher-ups knowing the identities of survey participants. By prioritizing privacy, we sought to create a space where every employee feels secure in sharing their thoughts, contributing to a more accurate representation of the organization’s happiness landscape.

üß™ Methods

Quantitative Surveys

The challenge in measuring employee engagement is the same as with any measurement related to complex human behaviors and perceptions. It must take into consideration a multitude of factors, all of which contribute to an overall understanding of individual engagement levels, which are then reflected in team and organizational views.

Survey-based models for measuring engagement range in specificity from three to more than 15 factors, but they all try to measure five basic engagement categories

  1. Current job understanding. The extent to which employees believe they know what is required to perform well in their current position; that they have received the right training, coaching, tools and resources to be successful; and that their work matters.

  2. Relationship with direct supervisor. The extent to which employees believe they have a good relationship with their manager; and that their manager understands their job and the issues they are facing, removes obstacles that may inhibit competent performance, and is looking out for their future growth and development.

  3. Perception of senior leadership. The extent to which employees believe that the senior leaders of the organization are capable and are moving the organization toward a better future (such as market dominance, achievement of financial targets or accomplishment of the organization’s mission).

  4. Opportunities for career growth and development. The extent to which employees believe that their stay with the organization can be more than just a short-term “job,” that they have the chance to develop their skills and have a longer-term career.

  5. Work conditions. The extent to which fundamental elements of the job or organization affect engagement, such as inadequate compensation or benefits, lack of enabling technologies, and workplace or organizational structural issues that inhibit efficient operations. Note that because these elements are not considered to be intrinsic motivational factors, they often don‚Äôt drive engagement positively when they are done well, but they can seriously detract from overall engagement when done poorly.

Qualitative Surveys

Prioritizing a comprehensive understanding of employee sentiments, we crafted a strategic approach to gather qualitative insights seamlessly. To delve deeper into the nuances of employee experiences, we initiated a series of follow-up surveys, allowing users to share their perspectives in an accessible and user-friendly manner. This preliminary step aimed to collect rich qualitative data before embarking on semi-guided interviews, ensuring a well-rounded exploration of their opinions and concerns. By employing this thoughtful methodology, we positioned ourselves to extract meaningful narratives, providing valuable context for our study.

I designed a follow-up survey that focused on areas of poor or declining performance in the quantitative surveys. With a series of more granular numeric questions and several short answer responses, I was able to gather more elaborate reasoning behind the employee’s ratings.

Focus Groups

Moderated discussion with a group of users, allowing insight into user attitudes, ideas, and desires. I brought on another senior engineer who had established ethos with the team and a more comprehensive view of the organization as a whole. we used the survey results.

The qualitative data analysis followed a systematic approach,

  1. Transcription: All interviews were transcribed accurately.
  2. Open Coding: Initial codes were generated by two independent coders through a line-by-line review of the transcripts. The coders met regularly to discuss and refine codes.
  3. Axial Coding: Codes were grouped into broader themes and subthemes, maintaining a constant dialogue between coders to ensure consensus.
  4. Memoing: Reflective memos were maintained throughout the coding process, documenting insights and questions.
  5. Data Reduction and Integration: Codes were selectively focused on to identify core elements related to the research objectives.
  6. Theory Development: Constant comparative analysis allowed for the refinement of emerging theories and concepts.
  7. Thematic Analysis: Overarching themes were extracted, and visual aids, such as thematic maps, were used for data display.
  8. Interpretation: Findings were interpreted within the context of the research objectives, existing literature, and theories.

ü§ì Learning

Embarking on my inaugural corporate professional UX research study, I encountered a wealth of insights that have profoundly shaped my approach as a researcher. This study provided a unique opportunity to navigate the intricacies of small group dynamics. I learned the art of establishing trust within these settings, fostering an environment where participants felt comfortable sharing their perspectives.

Navigating potentially heated discussions became a valuable skill as I sought to extract candid feedback. Advocating for less powerful voices emerged as a priority, ensuring that every participant felt heard and represented. This experience underscored the importance of inclusivity in the research process.

Furthermore, the study illuminated the significance of providing diverse avenues for data collection to accommodate varying comfort levels. I recognize this may not always be possible especially in studies that include several participants, however, when given the luxury of doing so, I do believe it is worth developing novel ways to engage study participants. Balancing the need for comprehensive insights with participant comfort became a cornerstone of my methodology. In essence, this project has not only expanded my proficiency in UX research but has instilled in me the importance of empathy, inclusivity, and adaptability in corporate research settings.
